## Chapter 1: Introduction – Wet Bulb Temperature in Early Warning Systems

The escalating threat of extreme heat events, driven by anthropogenic climate change, demands increasingly sophisticated tools for risk assessment and early warning systems. Traditional meteorological forecasts often rely on air temperature, a limited metric that fails to adequately capture the physiological impact of heat exposure. Wet bulb temperature (WBT), defined as the temperature a thermometer would read if covered by a wet cloth, offers a significantly more relevant indicator of human thermal discomfort and strain. Unlike air temperature, WBT incorporates the evaporative cooling effect of perspiration, providing a more accurate representation of the human body’s ability to dissipate heat. Consequently, WBT is rapidly gaining prominence as a critical component in early warning systems for heatwaves and related health risks [REF_1, REF_2].

This chapter provides an introduction to the significance of WBT in the context of early warning systems, outlining the fundamental physics behind its relevance and highlighting the growing research interest in its prediction. The selection of this topic is driven by the urgent need for improved forecasting capabilities to mitigate the adverse health consequences associated with prolonged exposure to high temperatures.  Understanding WBT’s relationship to human physiological response is paramount, as it directly reflects the potential for heat stress, including heat exhaustion and heatstroke. 

Recent advancements in machine learning, particularly deep learning techniques, have demonstrated promising results in WBT prediction [REF_3, REF_4].  Previous research, such as that by Chen et al. [REF_1], has established the groundwork for utilizing deep learning models for WBT prediction, while Vourloti et al. [REF_2] presented a robust methodology for calculating WBT from readily available surface observations – a crucial step for operational implementation.  Furthermore, Gao et al. [REF_3] and Gao et al. [REF_4] explored hybrid deep learning models and deep neural networks, respectively, showcasing the potential for enhanced predictive accuracy.  However, existing research is primarily focused on model development and validation, with limited discussion on the integration of WBT predictions into operational early warning systems and the challenges associated with data availability and model uncertainty. 

This chapter will synthesize the current state of research on WBT prediction, focusing on the methodologies employed, key findings regarding model performance, and the remaining critical gaps that must be addressed to translate these advancements into practical, reliable early warning systems.  The subsequent chapters will delve into specific aspects of WBT prediction, including data sources, model architectures, and validation strategies, ultimately aiming to contribute to the development of robust and effective tools for protecting public health during extreme heat events.
